# Bio-Medical Waste Management Rules, 2016 (as amended 2018 & 2019) — Reference Summary

Source: Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change (India), Bio-Medical
Waste Management Rules 2016, S.O. 1035(E), and the 2018/2019 amendments. This
document is a working reference summary for the Biomedical Waste Agent's
RAG-grounded classification — it is not a substitute for the gazetted rules
themselves, and any classification this agent produces should be treated as
decision support, not a compliance certification.

## 1. Applicability and duties of the occupier

The rules apply to every person who generates, collects, receives, stores,
transports, treats, disposes or handles bio-medical waste in any form, including
hospitals, nursing homes, clinics, dispensaries, veterinary institutions, animal
houses, pathological laboratories, blood banks, ayush hospitals, clinical
establishments, research or educational institutions, and health camps.

The "occupier" — in a hospital's case, the person in overall charge — is
responsible for: taking all steps to ensure bio-medical waste is handled without
adverse effect to human health and the environment; pre-treating (as applicable)
laboratory waste, microbiological waste, blood samples and blood bags through
disinfection or sterilisation on-site; phasing out chlorinated plastic bags and
gloves; segregating waste at the point of generation into colour-coded containers;
not mixing bio-medical waste with other waste streams; and maintaining records of
generation, collection, storage, treatment and disposal.

## 2. Categories and colour coding (Schedule I / Schedule II)

The 2016 rules replaced the earlier ten-category scheme with four colour-coded
categories, each with a distinct treatment pathway:

### Yellow category
Human anatomical waste (tissues, organs, body parts), animal anatomical waste,
soiled waste (items contaminated with blood or body fluids, including cotton,
dressings, plaster casts, bedding), expired or discarded medicines, chemical waste
(used or discarded chemicals from diagnostic/research activity), chemical
liquid waste, discarded linens and beddings contaminated with blood or body
fluid, and microbiology/biotechnology waste. Yellow-category infectious waste is
treated by **incineration**, or by plasma pyrolysis, or (for certain sub-streams
such as expired medicines) by encapsulation or a dedicated destruction process.
Chemical liquid waste requires neutralisation before disposal into effluent
treatment.

### Red category
Contaminated recyclable waste: tubing, bottles, intravenous sets, catheters,
urine bags, syringes without needles, and gloves. Treated by **autoclaving,
microwaving or hydroclaving**, followed by shredding, and then given for
recycling or further processing — never sent to a landfill without treatment.

### White (translucent) category
Waste sharps including metal implants — needles, syringes with fixed needles,
needle tips, scalpels, blades. Must be **autoclaved or dry-heat sterilised** and
then shredded or mutilated to prevent reuse. Sharps must never be
recapped, bent, or broken by hand before disposal.

### Blue category
Broken or discarded glassware, and metallic body implants. Treated by
**disinfection (autoclave/microwave/hydroclave) followed by recycling**.

### General (non-bio-medical) waste
Waste not falling into the four categories above — kitchen waste, packaging,
general office waste — is handled as solid waste under the separate Solid Waste
Management Rules and is not the subject of colour-coded bio-medical segregation.

## 3. Storage duration

Bio-medical waste must not be stored beyond the timeframes needed for it to
remain safe pending collection by an authorised Common Bio-medical Waste
Treatment Facility (CBWTF):

- Yellow and Red category: **48 hours**
- White and Blue category: **72 hours**
- General waste awaiting handover: **24 hours**, in line with routine municipal
  collection cycles.

Storage beyond these windows without special dispensation (e.g. cold storage
extending the yellow-category limit in specific, documented circumstances) is a
non-compliance and should be flagged, not silently extended.

## 4. Segregation, labelling and container standards

Containers and bags must be labelled with the bio-hazard symbol (for infectious
waste) or the cytotoxic hazard symbol (where applicable), be leak-proof, and use
puncture-proof containers for sharps. Bags must not be filled beyond three-
quarters capacity, must be sealed before transport within the facility, and must
never be compacted in a manner that could rupture them. Colour-coded bins must be
available at every point of generation — segregation is a point-of-generation
duty, not something to be corrected downstream.

## 5. Transportation and treatment facilities

Bio-medical waste must be transported only in vehicles authorised for the
purpose, and only to a facility holding a valid authorisation under these rules —
typically a Common Bio-medical Waste Treatment Facility (CBWTF) serving a cluster
of healthcare establishments, or a captive treatment facility for larger
institutions. Vehicles must carry a manifest (a "Bar-Coded" system was introduced
by the 2018 amendment for tracking bags from generation to disposal).

## 6. Reporting and authorisation

Every occupier must obtain prior authorisation from the State Pollution Control
Board (or Pollution Control Committee for Union Territories), submit an annual
report by the prescribed date each year, and maintain records for inspection.
Accidents involving bio-medical waste handling must be reported immediately to
the prescribed authority.

## 7. What this agent should say when it cannot classify confidently

If a described waste stream does not clearly match one of the four categories
above — for example, an unusual mixed-material item, or a description too vague
to assign with confidence — the correct response is to say so explicitly and
recommend manual review by the facility's biomedical waste officer, rather than
force a best-guess category. An incorrect colour-category assignment is a
compliance and safety risk, not a minor labelling error.
